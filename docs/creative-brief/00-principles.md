# Phase 0 — Principles

> The filter for everything below. Any site that violates a principle, however pretty, is out.
> These are not aspirations. They are cuts.

---

## Why principles first

Interior design lives in three dimensions the screen can't reproduce: touch, air, temperature, time-of-day light, scale-to-your-body, ambient sound, the way materials weather. A great interior-design website is not a photograph of the studio's work — it is a **translation** that preserves what survives the screen (composition, proportion, materiality, light quality, spatial hierarchy) and **compensates** for what dies (touch, temperature, sequence-in-space) through pacing, sound, motion, and reveal.

A site that only shows the work has understood nothing. A site whose interface competes with the work has actively harmed it. A site that unfolds like the studio's rooms unfold is the goal.

Everything below flows from that.

---

## The seven principles

### 1. The room reads first, the interface reads second

The site's chrome — nav, cursor, transitions, ornamentation — must recede so the work reads. Ornamented UI on interior-design work is like hanging a Rothko in a Baroque frame. If your eye lands on the interface before it lands on the space, the site has failed.

**Cuts:** heavy branded wrappers, decorative sidebar chrome, ambient motion that draws the eye off the photography, oversized navigation, "creative" cursors that dominate every hover.

---

### 2. Reveal, don't display

Great architecture unfolds sequentially — you enter, you turn, you notice. A grid dump of 40 thumbnails is a lie about how spaces work. The site must lead the eye one project at a time, one detail at a time, with intentional pacing between reveals.

**Cuts:** dense portfolio grids, "shop wall" layouts, sites that show every project on first scroll, above-the-fold information density.

---

### 3. Slowness is compositional

Interior design is contemplation, not consumption. Fast animations, rapid staggered reveals, and snappy timing betray the discipline. The site must give the eye time to rest **inside** each frame. Silences and pauses are load-bearing — they are not empty, they are compositional decisions.

**Cuts:** continuous scroll-linked animation, rapid staggered text, TikTok-pace section transitions, sites that never let the eye rest, "energetic" motion languages.

---

### 4. The camera respects the room

Photography is treated as **work**, not as texture. Every image is framed the way the designer intends the space to be understood — wide enough to show air, patient enough to show light. Never cropped to fit a UI gimmick.

**Cuts:** parallax zooms that break composition, aggressive image masking, hero images cropped to landscape strips regardless of subject, "cinemagraphs" that add motion to a still, gallery grids that force uniform aspect ratios across different intents.

---

### 5. Materiality lives in the work, never the wrapper

You evoke plaster, oak, brass through subtle color shifts and considered photographic exposure — never through decorative surface treatments. If the site's texture competes with the interior's texture, the site is decorating over the work.

**Cuts:** film-grain overlays, noise textures, "handmade paper" backgrounds, brushstroke ornament, wood-grain UI panels, plaster-textured section dividers, anything that says "look at the site's craft" instead of the studio's craft.

---

### 6. Motion is choreographed, not sprinkled

Every animation must communicate one specific thing (*this is important*, *this changed*, *look here*, *the page is transitioning*) or unify the site as a system. Decoration disguised as motion is banned. If you can't state what an animation communicates, cut it.

**Cuts:** random micro-interactions on every element, hover morphs on non-interactive things, magnetic buttons that magnetize for no reason, cursor-follower blobs without purpose, scroll-linked animation on decorative shapes.

---

### 7. The site must feel like one of their rooms

Brand, type, palette, motion, and pacing must collectively evoke the *temperature* of the studio's actual work. If the studio's projects are restrained and warm, a maximalist glowing site is lying about who they are. The alignment must be so tight you could describe the studio's design philosophy from the website alone.

**Cuts:** trendy visual languages disconnected from the studio's work, mismatches between the site's mood and the projects' mood, "current-year" aesthetics adopted for their own sake, sites that could be swapped between studios without loss.

---

## How to apply

Every site in Phase 1 must pass all seven. Every choice in Phase 4's Build Spec must be defensible against them. When two options conflict, keep the one that serves the interior design work; kill the one that only serves style.

If a Phase 1 pick is transcendent but violates a principle, the principle wins — either the pick goes, or the principle is rewritten with an argument for why. No silent exceptions.
